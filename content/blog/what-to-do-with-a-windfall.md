You know what's funny about getting a chunk of money all at once? It feels less real than money you earn slowly.

Your brain assigns different weight to different dollars. The $50 you worked for feels like $50. The $5,000 tax refund feels like Monopoly money. This is called "mental accounting" and it's the main reason windfalls evaporate.

People get a $3,000 bonus and spend it on things they'd never have bought with three months of careful saving. The money came fast, so it feels okay to let it go fast. It doesn't feel like a sacrifice. It just feels like... the natural order of things.

It's not. And that pattern is expensive.

## The 24-Hour Parking Lot

The single most effective thing you can do when money arrives unexpectedly: park it immediately in a savings account for 24-48 hours. Don't spend anything. Don't make any decisions. Just let it sit there.

This sounds almost insultingly simple. But it works because the urge to spend a windfall is highest in the first few hours. It feels like found money, and found money wants to be spent on something fun and immediate.

After 48 hours, the money starts to feel normal. It starts to feel like your money — not a surprise. And you start making different decisions about it.

## The Actual Split That Works

Once you've cooled down, try this split:

- **10-20% for fun.** Deliberately, consciously spend some on something you enjoy. This isn't failure — it's the safety valve that keeps you from resenting the other 80%. If you put 100% into investments, you'll break the rule within three months.

- **Any high-interest debt, first.** If you have credit card balances above 15%, every dollar you put there is a guaranteed 15-20% return. Nothing in the market beats that reliably.

- **The rest into your investment account.** Not "I'll do it next week." Today. Before you talk yourself out of it.

## The Part People Skip

Most people think they'll be disciplined about a windfall and then aren't — not because they're weak, but because they don't make the decision binding. They tell themselves they'll invest it "soon." They leave it in checking. They get a new impulse. They spend.

The move is to make the decision irreversible fast. Transfer the investment portion. Set up the extra 401k contribution. Send the debt payment. Do it while the intention is still fresh, because intention has a very short shelf life.

Windfalls are a shortcut. Used well, a single $10,000 windfall invested at 30 can be worth $75,000 by retirement. Used carelessly, it's a TV you forgot you bought.

The gap between those two outcomes is 48 hours and one wire transfer.
